The journey begins with the realization that light, possessing a complex dual nature, travels in straight lines until it hits a boundary. When light strikes opaque surfaces, it undergoes reflection, bouncing back symmetrically according to strict geometric laws. Spherical mirrors, whether concave (converging) or convex (diverging), manipulate these bouncing rays to magnify objects or vastly expand our field of view.\nWhen light hits transparent boundaries, it enters the material and suffers a change in velocity, leading to refraction. This bending phenomenon, perfectly predicted by Snell's Law and quantified by the material's Refractive Index, explains why swimming pools look shallower than they are, why diamonds sparkle through total internal reflection, and how thick glass slabs shift light rays sideways via lateral displacement.\nFinally, the intentional shaping of transparent materials gives rise to spherical lenses. Convex lenses converge light to project real images or act as magnifiers, while concave lenses diverge light to shrink images and correct eyesight. Through rigorous application of the New Cartesian Sign Convention, the mirror and lens formulas allow us to mathematically predict the exact location, size, and nature of any image in the universe.
