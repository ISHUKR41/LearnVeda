Highly polished and smooth surfaces, such as mirrors, reflect almost all the light that falls on them, creating a clear image.\n1\nThis behavior is guided by\nFermat's Principle\n, which states that light will always choose the path that takes the shortest amount of time to travel from one point to another.\n2\nThe Laws of Reflection\nWhenever reflection happens, it strictly follows two rules\n2\n:\nThe angle of incidence (the angle at which the light hits the surface) is always exactly equal to the angle of reflection (the angle at which the light bounces off).\nThe incident ray (incoming light), the reflected ray (bouncing light), and the normal (an imaginary straight line drawn exactly 90 degrees to the surface at the point of contact) all lie perfectly flat in the same plane.\nSpherical Mirrors Terminology\nInstead of flat mirrors, we often use curved mirrors made by cutting a piece from a hollow glass sphere.\n3\nConcave Mirror:\nThe mirror is curved inwards, like a cave. It spreads light rays apart (diverges them).\n1\nPole (P):\nThe exact center point of the mirror's surface.\nCenter of Curvature (C):\nThe center point of the original glass sphere from which the mirror was cut.\nPrincipal Axis:\nThe imaginary straight line passing directly through the Pole and the Center of Curvature.\nPrincipal Focus (F):\nThe specific point on the principal axis where light rays coming parallel to the axis actually meet (concave) or appear to meet (convex) after reflection.\n1\nFocal Length (\n):\nThe distance between the Pole and the Focus. By curving that mirror inward (Concave) or outward (Convex), we can use the strict geometric Laws of Reflection to manipulate the bouncing light.
